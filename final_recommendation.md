# Final Recommendation

# Executive Summary

Regression analysis was performed to identify the factors most strongly associated with monthly sales performance across stores. Three models were evaluated, including two simple regression models and one multiple regression model. The multiple regression model was selected as the final model because it explained approximately 81% of the variation in monthly sales and provided the strongest business insights.

---

# Factors Most Strongly Associated with Monthly Sales

The analysis indicates that the following variables are positively associated with monthly sales:

1. Footfall
2. Inventory Availability
3. Marketing Spend

Among these variables, footfall appears to be the strongest driver of sales performance.

The East region dummy variable showed a negative coefficient, indicating that stores located in the East region generate lower sales compared with the reference category (West region).

---

# Variables Leadership Should Focus On

## Customer Footfall

Footfall is the most important variable in the model. Higher customer traffic is associated with significantly higher sales.

Leadership should focus on:

* Increasing store visits.
* Improving customer acquisition.
* Running local marketing campaigns.
* Enhancing store experience.

---

## Inventory Availability

Inventory availability has a strong positive relationship with sales.

Leadership should focus on:

* Maintaining adequate stock levels.
* Reducing stockouts.
* Improving supply chain efficiency.

---

## Marketing Spend

Marketing expenditure contributes positively to sales.

Leadership should focus on:

* Allocating marketing budgets efficiently.
* Investing in promotions that increase customer traffic.
* Monitoring return on marketing investment.

---

# Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

The East region dummy variable should be interpreted carefully.

Although the model suggests lower sales in East region stores, the coefficient does not necessarily imply that the region itself causes lower sales. Other factors not included in the model may influence performance.

Similarly, the intercept value should not be interpreted literally because it represents a hypothetical situation where all predictors equal zero.

---

# Recommended Business Actions

Based on the regression results, the following actions are recommended:

1. Increase initiatives that improve customer footfall.
2. Maintain high inventory availability across stores.
3. Continue investing in marketing activities with measurable returns.
4. Investigate why East region stores underperform relative to West region stores.
5. Monitor store-level performance and identify hidden drivers not captured by the model.

---

# Risks and Limitations

Although the model explains approximately 81% of the variation in sales, it does not capture every factor influencing store performance.

The model does not include:

* Seasonality effects.
* Customer demographics.
* Pricing strategies.
* Local competition.
* Economic conditions.
* Promotional campaigns.
* Store management quality.

Therefore, prediction errors are expected and the results should be interpreted alongside business judgment.

---

# Association Does Not Automatically Mean Causation

Regression analysis identifies relationships between variables and sales, but it does not prove that one variable directly causes another.

For example, stores with high footfall tend to generate higher sales. However, this does not necessarily mean that footfall alone causes higher sales. Strong stores may simultaneously benefit from better locations, promotions, customer service, and product assortment.

Similarly, higher marketing spend may be associated with higher sales, but other unobserved factors may also contribute to performance.

Therefore, regression should be used as a decision-support tool rather than definitive proof of cause and effect.

---

# Final Recommendation

The multiple regression model should be used as the primary decision-support model.

The analysis suggests that leadership should prioritize:

* Increasing customer footfall.
* Maintaining inventory availability.
* Investing in marketing activities.

Among all factors studied, customer footfall appears to have the strongest association with monthly sales and should receive the greatest strategic attention.

Overall, the regression results provide strong evidence that operational improvements and customer acquisition initiatives can contribute to better store performance, while recognizing that regression analysis measures association and not absolute causation.
